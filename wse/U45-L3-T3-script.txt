Mongolians have long been famous for their horse riding prowess.

In the days of Genghis Khan, a fierce cavalry helped create one of the largest empires ever known.

Today, on the steppes of Mongolia, life has changed little over the centuries.

The horses still important to their semi nomadic way of life.

Each July, thousands come from all over Mongolia to the outskirts of Ulaanbatur for the festival of Naadam.

It's the premier forum for national competition in classic Mongolian sports, including horse racing.

The race is a little unusual.

To compete, riders must be under 12 years old.

On the day of the first race, preparations begin at dawn.

Horses are distinguished by a leather tail wrap, always wound clockwise.

Forelocks are also bound.

Mare's milk is offered to invoke a blessing from the sacred powers of nature.

A circle of incense purifies.

A drop of fermented milk protects from harm.

Parents circle in a staging area.

Some 500 riders will compete in this first race.

Before they can begin, the riders must walk the horses over 15 miles miles to the starting point.

From the finish line, it is impossible for spectators to see the drama unfolding miles away.

According to myth, the dust kicked up by winning horses showers happiness and prosperity on all those it touches.

Riders appear.

These front runners have already been galloping for nearly 30 minutes.

The blue sash of victory goes to the first five horses.

After the flurry of winners, racers trickle in for another hour.

Naadam concludes in the national stadium, a herald sings the praises of the winning horses as the champions take their victory lap.

Medals and mare's milk do them honor.

It is a proud moment, these youngsters have proven their skills in one of Mongolia's most important traditions.
